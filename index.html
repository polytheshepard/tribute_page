In Tribute to
Yukio Mishima
(Hiraoka Kimitake)

Biography

Yukio Mishima was a Japanese author, poet, and playwright. He was mainly known for forming the Tatenokai, a small militia group that attempted to restore Japan's national values which would eventually lead to his death through seppuku. In spite of his political stance, he was generally known for his novels and short novellas.

He was an internally conflicted person as it reflected from his father's refusal to accept his son's gift for writing. It was not until Mishima was physically exhausted from his position in the government's Finance Ministry that his father allowed him to pursue his passion for writing. He wrote his first controversial semi-autobiographical novel, Confessions of a Mask depicting a homosexual who must mask his sexual preferences from society. Through this initial success he proceeded to write his latter greater works, The Temple of the Golden Pavilion, The Sea of Fertility and Death in Midsummer and Other Stories.

He developed a writing style that would echo the dichotomy of the private life imbued with a character's contradicted throughts and feelings alongside with the expectations of society. This synergised his darker concepts of death, love and living where the changing force of Japan's current political environment was growing rapid. While Mishima lived a short life, he was a significantly occupied person despite of his difficult childhood. He took up acting having starred in the film Afraid to Die alongside directing his own films such as Yukoku. He also picked up weight training where his workout schedule included three sessions per week and continued for the last 15 years of his life. Mishima believed in living life to the fullest, as written in his final words before he committed seppuku:

Human life is limited but I would like to live forever.

-- Yukio Mishima

Timeline

January 14 1925 Yukio Mishima, or his real name Kimitake Hiraoka was born in Tokyo Japan to parents Azusa Hiraoka and Shizue Hiraoka.
Early childhood Mishima lived with his grandmother throughout his early childhood.
1931 Mishima was enrolled in an elite school, the Peer's School in Tokyo.
1937 Mishima began writing his stories whilst reading many classical Japanese Western authors including, Raymond Radiquet, Oscar Wilde and Rainer Maria Rilke.
1944 His short story piece,Hanazakari no Mori (Forest in Full Bloom) which described his feelings that his ancestors were still with him and was published in the literary magazine Bungei-Bunka.
1945 Mishima visited a famed writer Yasunari Kawabata in Kamakura taking with him manuscripts for Chusei (The Middles Ages) and Tabako whilst asking Kawabata for advice and assistance.
1946 Mishima began his first novel Tozoku (Thieves) which told a story about two young aristocratic members of society who are drawn towards suicide.
1946 He released another story Tabako (The Cigarette) describing his bulling at school after confessing that he was in the literary club to the rugby team.
1949 First published Confessions of a Mask which was successful and rose Mishima to fame.
1954 He would later provide more of his trauma in his story Shi no Kaku Shonen (The Boy Who Wrote Poetry).
1955 Mishima took weight training for the first time and never stopped his training regime for the last 15 years of his life.
1960 Mishima had a starring role in Yasuzo Maumura's film Afraid to Die along with other films Yukoku, Black Lizard and Hitokiri.
1967 Mishima enlisted in the Ground Self-Defense Force (GSDF) and underwent training.
1968 He formed the Tatenokai, a private militia composed of young students who studied martial and physical principles and shore to protect the Emperor of Japan.
November 25 1970 Mishima delivered a speech to inspire a coup d'etat in the Tokyo headquarters of the Eastern Command of the Japan Self-Defense Forces. His speech was to restore power of the emperor, however it failed and several minutes after returned to the commandant's office and performed seppuku.
